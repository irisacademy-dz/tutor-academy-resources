Digital Scavenger Hunt (Present Simple)

An interactive and communicative activity where students leverage online resources to practice the Present Simple tense by searching for information.

A1 A2
Grammar • Digital • Speaking

Lesson Plan Highlights:

  • Learning Objectives: Students will be able to form and use Present Simple sentences to describe facts and routines; practice scanning for specific information online.
  • Target Audience: Young learners to adults (A1-A2).
  • Materials: Internet-enabled devices (computers, tablets), projector (optional), worksheets with incomplete sentences.
  • Procedure Outline:
    1. Warm-up (5-10 min): "Find someone who..." mingler with simple present questions (e.g., "Find someone who wakes up before 7 AM").
    2. Activity (30-40 min): Students work in pairs or small groups. Provide them with a list of "People around the world..." sentences to complete (e.g., "In Japan, people usually eat _____ for breakfast." or "Most professional athletes train _____ hours a day."). Students use search engines to find the missing information, focusing on Present Simple.
    3. Discussion & Correction (10-15 min): Groups share their findings. Correct sentences and clarify any vocabulary or grammatical points.
    4. Extension (10-15 min): Students create their own "interesting facts" sentences using the Present Simple, which can be compiled into a class "fact book" or digital infographic.
  • Differentiation: Provide sentence starters for lower levels; encourage more complex sentence structures for higher levels.

Picture Dictation 2.0

A modern, tech-enhanced version of the classic picture dictation activity, perfect for developing descriptive language and listening skills.

A1
Vocabulary • Listening • Digital

Lesson Plan Highlights:

  • Learning Objectives: Students will be able to describe simple images using basic vocabulary and prepositions; understand spoken descriptions and reproduce images accurately.
  • Target Audience: Beginner learners (A1).
  • Materials: Digital whiteboards (e.g., Jamboard, Miro, Zoom whiteboard), prepared images (simple scenes with objects, people, actions).
  • Procedure Outline:
    1. Warm-up (5-7 min): Quick sketch game – Teacher says a basic noun (e.g., "house," "tree," "cat"), students quickly sketch it. Compare drawings.
    2. Activity (25-35 min): Divide students into pairs (or breakout rooms online). Partner A has a secret image, Partner B has a blank digital whiteboard. Partner A describes the image in detail (e.g., "There is a big red apple on the table. Next to the apple, there is a small blue box."). Partner B draws what they hear. Switch roles after 2-3 images.
    3. Review (10 min): Pairs compare their drawings to the original images. Discuss challenges and successful descriptions.
    4. Tech Tip: For online classes, utilize breakout rooms and screen sharing for pairs to work on their shared digital whiteboards.
  • Differentiation: Provide a word bank or sentence starters for lower levels; encourage more complex adjectives and adverbs for advanced A1 learners.

Grammar in Memes

Engage students with contemporary culture by teaching and practicing various verb tenses through popular meme formats.

A2
Grammar • Digital • Creative

Lesson Plan Highlights:

  • Learning Objectives: Students will be able to identify and correctly use target grammar structures (e.g., Past Simple, Present Perfect, Future with 'will/going to') in a creative context; understand humor and nuances in English memes.
  • Target Audience: Pre-intermediate learners (A2).
  • Materials: Projector, internet access, collection of appropriate memes demonstrating target grammar (e.g., "One does not simply walk into Mordor" for Present Simple; "Drake Hotline Bling" for comparisons), online meme generator (optional).
  • Procedure Outline:
    1. Warm-up (5-10 min): Show a few well-known memes and ask "What's the joke?" or "What do you understand from this meme?". Discuss their interpretations.
    2. Introduction (10 min): Introduce target grammar (e.g., Present Perfect). Show 2-3 memes that subtly use this tense. Analyze the meme text and how the grammar contributes to the meaning/humor.
    3. Activity (25-35 min): In pairs or small groups, provide students with a selection of meme templates (images) and a list of scenarios or themes. Challenge them to create their own memes using the target grammar structure. Encourage creativity and humor.
    4. Presentation & Voting (10-15 min): Groups present their memes. Conduct a class meme contest where students vote for the funniest, most original, or best grammar-focused meme.
  • Extension: Students find or create memes that represent other grammar points they've learned.

Survival English Role Play

Practical, scenario-based role-playing activities designed to equip students with essential communication skills for real-life situations.

A1
Speaking • Task-Based • Role Play

Lesson Plan Highlights:

  • Learning Objectives: Students will be able to use basic English phrases to ask for help, give personal information, and navigate common everyday situations.
  • Target Audience: Beginner learners (A1), focusing on functional language.
  • Materials: Scenario cards (e.g., "lost phone," "doctor visit," "ordering food," "asking for directions"), language frames/sentence starters (e.g., "Excuse me, I need help with...", "Can you tell me where...?", "I'm looking for...").
  • Procedure Outline:
    1. Warm-up (5-10 min): "What would you do if..." - present simple emergency situations (e.g., "What do you do if you lose your passport?"). Brainstorm initial responses.
    2. Vocabulary & Phrases (10-15 min): Introduce key vocabulary and useful phrases for the given scenarios. Practice pronunciation and intonation.
    3. Activity (30-40 min): Divide students into pairs or small groups. Assign each group a scenario card and corresponding roles (e.g., tourist and helpful local; patient and doctor). Provide language frames as support. Students practice their role plays.
    4. Performance & Feedback (10-15 min): Selected pairs perform their role plays for the class. Provide constructive feedback on clarity, fluency, and appropriate language use.
    5. Extension: Students create their own survival English scenarios and write short dialogues.
  • Differentiation: Provide more explicit scripts for lower levels; encourage improvisation for more confident learners.

Verb Tense Timeline Challenge

An engaging, interactive timeline activity designed to help students visualize and correctly apply various verb tenses in context.

A2 B1
Grammar • Visual • Collaborative

Lesson Plan Highlights:

  • Learning Objectives: Students will be able to differentiate between and accurately use Past Simple, Present Perfect, and Past Continuous tenses to narrate events in a chronological order.
  • Target Audience: Pre-intermediate to intermediate learners (A2-B1).
  • Materials: Large paper or digital whiteboard, sticky notes or digital "cards" with events/actions, markers/digital pens.
  • Procedure Outline:
    1. Warm-up (5-10 min): "When I was 10..." chain story. Students take turns adding a sentence about their past, building a narrative. (e.g., "When I was 10, I loved ice cream. My friend bought me a cone...").
    2. Introduction (10-15 min): Review the concepts of different verb tenses and their placement on a timeline (past, present, future). Emphasize how certain tenses connect events.
    3. Activity (30-40 min): Divide the class into teams. Provide each team with a set of "event cards" (e.g., "I started learning English," "I graduated high school," "I have visited 5 countries," "I was sleeping when the phone rang"). Their task is to arrange these events on a timeline, writing a sentence for each using the most appropriate verb tense.
    4. Review & Discussion (10-15 min): Teams present their timelines and justify their tense choices. Discuss any discrepancies and clarify tense usage.
    5. Tech Tip: Use collaborative online whiteboards like Jamboard or Miro for teams to create and share their digital timelines.
  • Differentiation: Provide cues on cards (e.g., "use Present Perfect here") for lower levels; introduce more complex tenses (e.g., Past Perfect) for B1 learners.

5-Minute Debate Starters

A collection of quick, engaging opinion activities designed to get students speaking, sharing ideas, and developing their fluency and critical thinking.

A2 B1
Speaking • Critical Thinking • Debate

Lesson Plan Highlights:

  • Learning Objectives: Students will be able to express opinions, agree/disagree, and provide simple justifications in English; practice active listening and turn-taking in discussions.
  • Target Audience: Pre-intermediate to intermediate learners (A2-B1).
  • Materials: List of debate prompts (e.g., "Summer is better than winter," "Dogs are better pets than cats," "Homework should be abolished"), timer.
  • Procedure Outline:
    1. Warm-up (3-5 min): "Stand up if you agree..." - Teacher states a simple opinion (e.g., "Pizza is the best food."), students physically respond by standing up if they agree.
    2. Introduction (5 min): Introduce sentence starters for expressing opinions and agreement/disagreement (e.g., "I think...", "In my opinion...", "I agree because...", "I don't agree. I think...").
    3. Activity (20-30 min): Present a debate topic. Divide the class into two sides (for and against). Give them 2-3 minutes to brainstorm arguments. Then, facilitate a short, structured debate (e.g., 1 minute per side for opening statements, 2 minutes for rebuttal, 1 minute for closing). Rotate topics every 5-7 minutes.
    4. Feedback (5-10 min): Provide brief feedback on language use and participation. Encourage students to reflect on strong arguments.
    5. Extension: Students record short video responses expressing their opinion on a topic.
  • Differentiation: Provide more explicit argument points for lower levels; challenge higher levels to use more complex linking words and rhetorical devices.

Emoji Storytelling

A fun and creative way to kickstart language production, where students construct narratives using only emojis.

A1 A2
Creative • Speaking • Warm-ups

How to:

  • Learning Focus: Narrative building, vocabulary recall, imaginative thinking.
  • Procedure:
    1. Teacher displays 3-5 emojis in a sequence on the screen (e.g., 🚀➡️👽➡️🍕).
    2. In pairs or small groups, students must create a short story that connects all the emojis in order. Encourage them to be imaginative.
    3. Groups share their stories with the class.
    4. Variation: Students create their own emoji sequences for other groups to interpret.
  • Online Adaption: Use screen sharing for emojis, and chat/breakout rooms for story creation.

Word Association Chain

A fast-paced vocabulary activation game that encourages quick thinking and expands lexical networks.

A1 A2 B1
Vocabulary • Fast-paced • Warm-ups

How to:

  • Learning Focus: Vocabulary recall, semantic networks, quick thinking.
  • Procedure:
    1. Teacher starts with a word (e.g., "ocean").
    2. The next student immediately says a word associated with the previous one (e.g., "fish").
    3. The game continues around the circle. Words cannot be repeated.
    4. A student is out if they hesitate for too long or repeat a word.
  • Add Challenge: Introduce a topic (e.g., "food," "travel") to keep associations within a specific theme.
  • Tech Option: Use the chatbox for an online version, students type their words quickly.

Silent Ball Grammar Review

A dynamic, kinesthetic activity for reviewing specific grammar points, promoting active participation and immediate application.

A2 B1
Grammar • Active • Warm-ups

How to:

  • Learning Focus: Grammar review (e.g., Past Simple, Present Perfect, Conditionals), sentence construction, quick recall.
  • Procedure:
    1. Students stand in a circle. The teacher introduces a target grammar point (e.g., "Today we'll practice Past Simple sentences.").
    2. The teacher tosses a soft ball to a student. That student must immediately say a grammatically correct sentence using the target tense.
    3. They then toss the ball to another student, who must say a new sentence in the target tense.
    4. If a student makes a mistake or hesitates for too long, they're "out" or must complete a quick challenge.
  • Add Challenge: Specify a theme or a vocabulary word that must be included in the sentence.
  • Online Adaption: Use a virtual ball toss app or simply call on students rapidly.
